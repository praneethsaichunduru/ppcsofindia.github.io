Sardar Sarovar Project

The Sardar Sarovar Project (SSP) is a multipurpose river valley project constructed on the Narmada River in the state of Gujarat, India. It is one of the largest water resource projects in India and plays a crucial role in irrigation, drinking water supply, and hydroelectric power generation. The project was first envisioned in 1946, and after decades of planning and legal battles, construction began in 1987. The Sardar Sarovar Dam, a key component of this project, was finally completed in 2017 and dedicated to the nation by Prime Minister Narendra Modi. The primary aim of this project is to harness the water of the Narmada River to provide irrigation, drinking water, and electricity to multiple states, including Gujarat, Rajasthan, Maharashtra, and Madhya Pradesh.

The Sardar Sarovar Dam is a concrete gravity dam constructed using roller-compacted concrete (RCC) technology, making it one of the largest structures of its kind in the world. The dam stands at a height of 163 meters, with a length of 1,210 meters, and is designed to withstand massive water pressure. The spillway of the dam consists of 30 radial gates, ensuring effective flood control and water management. The powerhouse located at the dam site consists of two hydroelectric powerhouses: a canal head powerhouse with an installed capacity of 250 MW and a riverbed powerhouse with a capacity of 1,200 MW, collectively generating substantial renewable energy. The construction of the dam involved extensive geological surveys, advanced engineering techniques, and the use of high-quality cement and reinforcements to ensure durability and stability.

The Sardar Sarovar Reservoir, also known as the Narmada Reservoir, has a gross storage capacity of 9.5 billion cubic meters (BCM) and a live storage capacity of 5.8 BCM. The full reservoir level is 138.68 meters, and it covers an area of approximately 37,000 hectares. The project ensures water supply to over 75% of Gujarat's population, benefiting nearly 9,000 villages and several cities, including Ahmedabad, Rajkot, and Bhavnagar. The water from the dam is distributed through an extensive network of canals, including the Narmada Main Canal, which is the longest irrigation canal in the world, stretching over 532 kilometers. The project has significantly enhanced groundwater recharge and has helped in mitigating the adverse effects of drought-prone regions in western India.

The Sardar Sarovar Project plays a vital role in the agricultural development of Gujarat and neighboring states. The irrigation potential of the project covers approximately 1.8 million hectares of land, benefiting farmers in Gujarat, Rajasthan, and Maharashtra. The canal system ensures that arid and semi-arid regions receive a reliable water supply, thereby improving crop yields and agricultural productivity. The availability of water has encouraged farmers to diversify their crops, shifting from traditional rain-fed farming to high-value crops such as fruits, vegetables, and oilseeds. Moreover, the project has helped in controlling soil erosion and improving soil fertility through efficient water management practices. The introduction of modern irrigation techniques, such as drip and sprinkler irrigation, has further optimized water usage and minimized wastage.

The Sardar Sarovar Project has also played a crucial role in socio-economic development, providing employment opportunities to thousands of people during its construction phase and continuing to support local livelihoods through agriculture and industries. The project has facilitated the growth of agro-based industries, fisheries, and tourism in the region. However, it has also faced significant criticism due to its environmental and social impacts, particularly the displacement of indigenous communities. The rehabilitation and resettlement of affected families have been a major challenge, with numerous protests and legal battles led by activists like Medha Patkar under the Narmada Bachao Andolan. Despite these challenges, the project remains a key component of India’s water resource management strategy, contributing significantly to the nation's agricultural, energy, and drinking water needs.
